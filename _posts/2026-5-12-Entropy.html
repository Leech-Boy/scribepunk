Food is essential to life, therefore, make it good. - Truett Cathy

In a closed system, entropy increases with time. - The Second Law of Thermodynamics

Many years ago, I read Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep, by Philip K. Dick. To those who don’t know it, it is a short sci-fi novel that provided the basis for the movie Blade Runner. In the story, there is a character who lives by himself in an abandoned apartment building. The building is all falling apart, but he keeps his room as neat as he can. He describes this as a war against “kippleization”. As time passes, all things tend to break down into unremarkable bits and pieces, kipple. After reading this story I began to notice, or at least have the words to describe this phenomenon.

Things break, and when they do, you either repair them, or throw them away. Throwing things away is the clearest example of kippleization. It goes onto a heap of waste where it will slowly degrade. It’s mere placement there is enough to declare it kipple. Even when you repair things, kippleization still occurs. Broken parts may be discarded. Even if they are not, there will be wear and tear on the tools that are used to do the fixing. Every time you use a tool to fix something, you transfer a bit of it’s brokenness to the tool. If reflection on this makes you think that maybe everything is always breaking, and the number of things that are broken can never really decrease, you would be right! In physics this idea is called “entropy”, and it is roughly defined as the number of equivalent states of a system. An example should clarify what this means. Suppose you want to measure the entropy of your bedroom. Your bedroom contains only a few loose articles of clothing, a hat, a shirt, and a sock, and a laundry basket they are supposed to go in. If all the pieces of clothing are in the laundry basket the entropy of your room is low. In order for this circumstance to arise, the sock must be in the laundry basket, the hat must be in the laundry basket, and the shirt must be in the laundry basket. Either all three of those things are true or they are not. So, there is one equivalent state. I expect this may still be confusing, so consider a second scenario. Suppose instead of all the clothing items resting in the laundry basket, one is on the floor. There are three ways this can be true, either the hat is on the floor, or the sock is on the floor, or the shirt is on the floor. There are three equivalent states. In this simple example, the entropy of the room when all clothes are in the laundry basket is the same as the entropy of the room when all clothes are on the floor. If we were to consider the probability of any particular item randomly landing on the floor versus in the laundry basket this would change, but hopefully you are starting to understand what entropy measures. Slightly more generally, you can think about how much you could rearrange the molecules in something before it started to seem very different. The molecules in a hammer can be rearranged many different ways without impacting the function of the hammer. The same is not true of a surgical scalpel. A relatively small change of the arrangement of molecules in a scalpel could render it unable to cut effectively. It would take a serious rearrangement of molecules for a hammer to not work properly. Therefore, the scalpel has a lower entropy than a hammer. The Second Law of Thermodynamics states that “Entropy increase with time in a closed system”. In other words, if you want to decrease entropy (i.e. increase order) you have to add something from outside the system, or remove something from the system. To decrease the entropy of your room, you must add yourself to the room and do the work of cleaning it up. Left alone, the things in the room will only degrade more and more. As it is written “Breath of breath. All is vain.” To put your life in order, you must always be bringing new things in and throwing old things out. You fix your car and your home, you fill your closet and your fridge, and all of this comes at a price. In order to keep order among the things you control you must always be spending money. The inflow of money is the thing that defines your non-closed system. Because money flows in, order can be kept. This is how I felt for a long time, and it was very discouraging. Nobody likes to think about spending money, especially for the basics. Lately I realized that it was not only discouraging, it was wrong. As an analogy for entropy, the flow of money works well, but it is just that, an analogy. The entropy of the things I control cannot be measured solely by the way that I spend my money. Physically speaking, money is just pieces of information that compels people to help me. The thing that enters the system of my life, physically speaking, is food. Food is the thing that gives me energy to go to work and earn a paycheck, and also to do the labor at home of keeping things together. Sometimes it is just my energy that is required, not money at all. Now if food gives me energy to earn money and money helps me get food, it seems hard to say which is the one that is really entering the system of my life, both bring the other along. The answer to this riddle is found by considering the nature of food. Food comes from farmers. Farmers eat food to gain energy, then they farm and make more food. It would seem that they are decreasing entropy, which as we know, is only possible if something is coming into the system. It can’t be food, because the food is created inside the system of farmer and their plants. Obviously, the thing entering the system is sunlight. Farmer’s work would be fruitless if the Sun didn’t provide the energy to grow the plants. In order for the Sun to grow plants (i.e. decrease their entropy), its own entropy must increase. As plants grow, the Sun is dying. When you eat food, the entropy of your life can decrease, at the cost of the Sun’s entropy increasing. When I think about this, it makes me happy. Instead of viewing my job as the thing that keeps my life together, I think about food being the thing that keeps my life together. Nobody likes to think about work outside of work. Everybody likes to think about food. The eating of food should be a joyful experience that is approached with a kind of reverence, or even awe. Food is the fundamental force at work in your life. Food is the only thing standing between yourself and the relentless forces of kippleization. Celebrate your food, eat with joy. By eating food you can defy the Second Law of Thermodynamics itself.

Eat, drink, and take pleasure in your toil. This is God’s gift to man. Ecclesiastes 3:13
