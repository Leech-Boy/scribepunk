For a long time, the way that I stored my passwords was very piecemeal. Some passwords were saved in my browser, some were saved to my Google password manager, others were saved in Google Docs, or even in text files on my phone. In September of last year, I consolidated all of my passwords into one place, a dedicated password manager called Bitwarden.

A password manager is exactly what it sounds like. It is a single place to store all of your passwords. You can access it on any computer that the Bitwarden application is downloaded on or on any browser with the Bitwarden plugin installed. All you have to do is enter a single, master password to access all your others, so you only ever need to memorize one password. This tech tip article is likely less applicable than the first one I wrote because it does not describe a solution to a problem with clear and present symptoms. The issue at stake is privacy, security, peace of mind, and independence. I am generally distrustful of large tech companies, perhaps for good reason, and I dislike the idea of them having access to my login credentials to all of the online services that I use. A quick Bing search about Google Password Manager did not reveal any claims by Google that they are unable to read your saved passwords, so I think it is safe to assume that they can, otherwise they would make that a selling point. I assume the same is true of most password managers that are built into systems for free, whether that is your browser, your operating system, or some other tool like a Google account. If someone were able to gain high enough access to the password databases of Google, or Apple, or your favorite web browser, they would be able to access every username and password saved to those databases by every user of that services’ password manager. Obviously, this would be very, very hard, but it is not impossible. A password manager on the other hand is much more secure (assuming you chose a reputable password manager). Even if a hacker were to gain complete access to every computer owned by the password manager company, they would not be able to access your passwords. The way that this works is a fascinating bit of cryptography that is described at the end. In order to access your passwords, the hackers would have to develop an update to the program, and assuming they have control of the lead developer’s computers, approve that update to be sent out. The users of the program would then have to install the update and use the infected version of the program in order for their passwords to be leaked. Supply chain attacks like this are possible but clearly this is a more complicated matter than gaining access to a database. Maybe you don’t need to worry about this. Maybe Apple’s password manager will never be breached. It’s fine. Just keep living your life. As for me, the mere fact that these big tech companies can just read your passwords that they help you save makes me uncomfortable enough to shell out a few bucks. That being said, Bitwarden is not free. When I subscribed, I paid $10 for the year, as of writing this, it is $19.80 for the year, not too bad. There is also a free version that is very good. You can use it on as many devices as you want, store as many passwords as you want, and share your passwords with one other user. The downside of this option is if you forget your master password, your account becomes unrecoverable. With the premium plan, you are able to give other Bitwarden users the ability to gain emergency access to your vault, allowing you to recover all your data (presumably Bitwarden would allow you to recreate your vault from scratch using a new password, but I have not verified that). There are of course plenty of other reasons you would want to allow other users to have emergency access to your passwords, allowing your family to access all your accounts when you die comes first to mind. Bitwarden runs on every major operating system and has plugins for most browsers. I no longer have passwords saved in five separate places. All my passwords are saved in Bitwarden or not at all, and my Bitwarden master password is saved in my brain. I only ever need to remember this one password. One of the benefits of this is that I never have to worry about having duplicated passwords. When signing up for a new service, I use Bitwarden’s password generator to create a new, random password, and save that to my vault. There are of course password manager besides Bitwarden, but when I was first shopping, it was once of the cheapest. It also seems the most trustworthy. The source code for Bitwarden is open source, meaning anybody can read the code that Bitwarden runs to verify that it is in fact doing what they claim. This and their extensive documentation on how their program achieves zero-knowledge of your passwords makes me feel like they are trustworthy.

How Bitwarden Works

This section of the article is much more technical and will likely not be as interesting as the introduction to the general public. I will make it as accessible as possible, even to the point of obscuring some unimportant technical details. To understand how Bitwarden can store your passwords without knowing what they are, requires you to understand “asymmetric key cryptography”. In asymmetric key cryptography, everyone who wants to communicate securely has a pair of keys. These keys are very large numbers that are hundreds of digits long. The important thing about these keys is that they come in pairs. One is designated the public key, the other is the private key. There is no special quality that causes one to be public versus private, it only matters that this distinction is kept consistent. The second important thing about these keys is that they can be used to encrypt or decrypt information. Any piece of data that is encrypted with one of these keys can only be decrypted by someone who holds the other key. Typically, this is used to send secure messages. If Alice wants to send a secret message to Bob, she uses his public key, which is public knowledge, to encrypt her message. The only person who can decrypt the message is the person who holds Bob’s private key, which should just be Bob. When Alice saves a password with Bitwarden, what happens behind the scenes is that the Bitwarden application on her computer encrypts the password that she wants to save, and sends it to the Bitwarden database to be stored when she wants to access it from another device. When this password is encrypted, it is encrypted so that only she can read it, you can imagine that she used her public key to encrypt it, since she is the only one with the corresponding private key, only she can decrypt it (technically this is incorrect, but it gets the idea across). When this encrypted password goes across the internet and into the Bitwarden database, nobody on the internet, nor even the Bitwarden developers can access this password, because they don’t have the right key to decrypt it. It is important to note that Alice’s public and private keys are not stored on her computer, or in any Bitwarden database. They are generated by the computer every time Alice signs in to her Bitwarden account on her computer. They are generated from her username and password so they are always the same. You can imagine that the Bitwarden application converts her username and password into numbers and multiplies them together to get her private key and constructs the corresponding public key that is the reverse key (in reality it is much more complicated, but this should communicate the basic idea). All that Bitwarden ever sees is Alice’s username and the encrypted passwords she saves. So, if Bitwarden doesn’t know anything about your encryption keys, and they aren’t saved anywhere, how does emergency account access work? It’s a very clever piece of cryptography, that I will explain briefly. When Alice gives Bob permission to request emergency access to her vault, this is what happens under the hood. Alice encrypts her private key using Bob’s public key and sends that result to the Bitwarden database. This encrypted key cannot be read by anyone but Bob. Bob cannot access it though because it is stored in the Bitwarden database, and the database will not give it to him. If he were able to get it, he could decrypt all of Alice’s passwords, assuming that the Bitwarden database allowed him to access those too. When Bob requests emergency access to Alice’s vault, Bitwarden sends a message to Alice, in the form of an email or text, informing her of Bob’s request. If Alice does not respond in a certain time frame, say 48 hours, to say that there is no emergency Bob will be given access to Alice’s vault. What this means is that Bitwarden will send Bob the Alice’s private key which was encrypted with his public key. Since Bob knows his own private key, he can decrypt this. Bob now knows Alice’s private key. Bitwarden will also send all of Alice’s encrypted passwords to Bob, which he can decrypt now that he has Alice’s private key. I think this is really cool, which is why I wrote this piece. The long and the short of it is, Bitwarden is just about as safe as any password manager can be. Of course, as one of my professors in college liked to say “The best way to protect your computer is to turn it off, and drop it in the middle of the ocean in shark-infested waters”.
